\section{Smoothing B-splines}

The Particle-Mesh coupling algorithms are based on a spline smoothing of the 
particle Dirac distribution in the discrete Lagrangian functional.
Let us first recall the definition of cardinal B-splines.

\subsection{Cardinal B-splines}
\label{sec:card_B_splines}

The cardinal B-spline of degree $d$ centered at $x=0$ can be defined
recursively using the following convolution
\begin{align*}
S^d(x) &= S^0 * S^{d-1} (x) = \int_{-\frac{1}{2}}^{\frac{1}{2}} S^{d-1} (x-x') \dd x'
\end{align*}
with 
\begin{align*}
S^0(x) &= \begin{cases}
     1  &\text{ if } ~ -\frac{1}{2} \leq x \leq \frac{1}{2} \\ 
     0 & \text{ else} 
          \end{cases}
\end{align*}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The cardinal splines are even ($S^d(x) = S^d(-x)$)
    \item The support of $S^d$ is $\left[-\frac{d+1}{2},\frac{d+1}{2}\right]$
    \item The support of $S^d_h$ is $\left[-\frac{d+1}{2}h,\frac{d+1}{2}h\right]$ 
    \item Both the
    reference and scaled cardinal splines have unit integral:
    $$ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} S^d(x) \dd x = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} S_h^d(x) \dd x = 1$$
    \item Denoting grid nodes by $x_i = ih$, the scaled cardinal B-spline verifies
    \begin{equation} \label{eq:splinerel}
        \int_{x_{i-\frac{1}{2}}}^{x_{i+\frac{1}{2}}} S_h^{d-1}(x-x_p)\dd x = hS_h^d(x_i-x_p) = S^d\Big(\frac {x_i-x_p}{h}\Big)
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Particle B-splines}
\label{sec:part_splines}

Associated with a particle with arbitrary position 
$\mathbf{x}_p = (x_p,y_p, z_p)$, we may define the \textbf{particle spline} as a tensor product B-spline
\begin{equation} 
    \label{eq:S_part}
    S^\part_h( \mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p) = S_{\Delta x}^{d_x-1}(x-x_p)S_{\Delta y}^{d_y-1}(y-y_p)S_{\Delta z}^{d_z-1}(z-z_p)
\end{equation}
involving univariant scaled splines of the form $S_{\Delta x}^d(x)=\frac {1}{\Delta x} S^d(\frac{x}{\Delta x})$.
These scaled B-splines transform the properties of the cardinal B-spline $S^d$ (corresponding to $h=1$) to equidistant grids with $h\neq1$. 

The particle B-spline is centered on a particle.

Using this particle B-spline, we can define a smoothed particle distribution function in phase space, of the form
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:f_part}
    f(t,\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}) = \sum_p w_p S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p(t))\delta (\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{v}_p(t))
\end{equation}
and smoothed charge and current particle densities in configuration space
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:rho_J_part}
    \rho(t,\mathbf{x}) = \sum_p q_p w_p S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p(t)), 
    ~~~~ 
    \bJ(t,\mathbf{x}) = \sum_p q_p w_p \mathbf{v}_p(t) S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p(t)).
\end{equation}


\subsection{Node and cell splines at particle positions}
\label{sec:node_cell_splines}

The coupling of particles and fields (as described below) involves two kinds of univariate B-splines, attached to grid nodes and cells
and evaluated at particle positions:
\begin{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{node splines} centered on grid nodes, of the form
    $$ 
    S^\node_i(x_p) := S^d \Big(\frac{x_p-x_i}{h}\Big)
    $$
    This spline is supported on the interval $\left[x_i -\frac{(d+1)h}{2}, x_i + \frac{(d+1)h}{2} \right]$,
    and its values are independent of the grid size.

    \item \textbf{cell splines} centered on cell midpoints, of the form
    $$ 
    S^\cell_i(x_p) := \frac 1h S^{d-1}\Big(\frac{x_p - x_\iph}{h}\Big)
    $$
    This spline is centered on the supported on the interval $\left[x_\iph -\frac{dh}{2}, x_\iph + \frac{dh}{2} \right]$,
    and its cell-integrals are independent of the grid size.

\end{itemize}
These splines naturally appear in the variational scheme since the above relation \ref{eq:splinerel} implies that 
the dual-node (resp. dual-cell) degree of freedom of a univariate particle spline is a cell (resp. node) spline evaluated at the particle position.


\section{Reduction of particle splines}
\label{sec:reduction_part_splines}

The reduction operators on the dual mesh for our smoothing function constructed from the cardinal B-Spline can be computed based on the properties above. 
Specifically, dual geometric degrees of freedom are products of univariate cell and node splines evaluated at the particles:
$$
\begin{aligned}
    \tcR_0(S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p))_{\iph,\jph,\kph} &= S^\cell_i(x_p)\,S^\cell_j(y_p) \, S^\cell_k(z_p)\\
    \tcR_1(\mathbf{e}_x S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p))_{i,\jph,\kph} &= S^\node_i(x_p)\,S^\cell_j(y_p) \, S^\cell_k(z_p)\\
    \tcR_1(\mathbf{e}_y S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p))_{\iph,j,\kph} &= S^\cell_i(x_p)\,S^\node_j(y_p) \, S^\cell_k(z_p)\\
    \tcR_1(\mathbf{e}_z S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p))_{\iph,\jph,k} &= S^\cell_i(x_p)\,S^\cell_j(y_p) \, S^\node_k(z_p)\\
    \tcR_2(\mathbf{e}_x S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p))_{\iph,j,k} &= S^\cell_i(x_p)\,S^\node_j(y_p) \, S^\node_k(z_p)\\
    \tcR_2(\mathbf{e}_y S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p))_{i,\jph,k} &= S^\node_i(x_p)\,S^\cell_j(y_p) \, S^\node_k(z_p)\\
    \tcR_2(\mathbf{e}_z S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p))_{i,j,\kph} &= S^\node_i(x_p)\,S^\node_j(y_p) \, S^\cell_k(z_p)\\
    \tcR_3(S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p))_{i,j,k}                &= S^\node_i(x_p)\,S^\node_j(y_p) \, S^\node_k(z_p).
\end{aligned}
$$

\section{Deposition (reduction) of particle densities }
\label{sec:deposition_of_part_densities}

In GEMPIC, the charge and current particle densities \ref{eq:rho_J_part}
play the role of Maxwell source terms through their dual degrees of freedom 
on the grid. 

Specifically, the particle charge density $\rho$ is represented as a 3-form on the dual mesh which is 
characterized by the dual cell integrals $\tilde{\arr{\rho}} = \tcR_3(\rho)$ in $\tilde{ \cC}_3$,
By linearity of the reduction operators, these degrees of freedom %(the dual cell integrals) 
read
$$
\tilde{\arr{\rho}}_{i,j,k}(t) := \tcR_3(\rho(t))_{i,j,k}
= \sum_p q_p w_p S^\node_i(x_p(t))\, S^\node_j(y_p(t)) \,S^\node_k(z_p(t)).
$$
As for the particle current density $\bJ$, it is represented as a 2-form on the dual mesh. 
Thus it is characterized 
by its fluxes through all the faces of the mesh, gathered in the vector 
$\tilde{\arrJ} = \tcR_2( \mathbf{J})$ in $\tilde{ \cC}_2$. These coefficients read
    % imilarly the coefficients of $\mathbf{J}$ in $\tilde{ \cC}_2$ are 
$$
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{\arrJ}^{x}_{\iph,j,k}(t) &:= \tcR_2( \mathbf{J}^{x}(t))_{\iph,j,k} = \sum_p q_p w_p v_{p,x}(t) S^\cell_i(x_p(t))\, S^\node_j(y_p(t)) \,S^\node_k(z_p(t)) \\ 
\tilde{\arrJ}^{y}_{i,\jph,k}(t) &:= \tcR_2( \mathbf{J}^{y}(t))_{i,\jph,k} =\sum_p q_p w_p v_{p,y}(t) S^\node_i(x_p(t))\, S^\cell_j(y_p(t)) \,S^\node_k(z_p(t)) \\
\tilde{\arrJ}^{z}_{i,j,\kph}(t) &:= \tcR_2( \mathbf{J}^{z}(t))_{i,j,\kph} = \sum_p q_p w_p v_{p,z}(t) S^\node_i(x_p(t))\, S^\node_j(y_p(t)) \,S^\cell_k(z_p(t)) \\
\end{aligned}
$$
which correspond respectively to the three components of $\mathbf{J}$, i.e.  $J_x$, $J_y$, $J_z$ 

\section{Smoothed electromagnetic fields}

The discrete electric field $\bE$ is characterized by its integrals on the edges of the primal grid, which can be found 
in Section~\ref{sec:dofs_and_reductions}.

One could define (for instance using a finite element setting) an electric field that is defined everywhere and 
has exactly these geometric degrees of freedom. 
This is, however, not necessary: Indeed, in the GEMPIC scheme the particles are coupled with a 
spline-smoothed electric field $\bE^S$ which only involves the above primal degrees of freedom $\arrE \in \cC_1$
and the reduction $\tcR_2$ of the (vectorized) particle splines, namely:
$$
E^S_d(\mathbf{x}_p)  
:=  \arrE \cdot \tcR_2\big(\mathbf{e}_{d} \, S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p)\big)
= \sum_{\texttt{e} \in \Big\{\substack{\text{edges} \\ \text{parallel to $\mathbf{e}_{d}$}}\Big\}} \arrE_{\texttt{e}}^d \tcR_2\big(\mathbf{e}_{d} \, S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p)\big)_{\tilde{\texttt{f}}(\texttt{e})}
$$
for all dimensions $d \in \{x,y,z\}$, 
where $\mathbf{e}_{d}$ is the canonical basis vector of $\mathbb{R}^3$ along dimension $d$,
and for each primal edge $\texttt{e}$ we have denoted by $\tilde{\texttt{f}}(\texttt{e})$ its dual face.
Given the reduction formulas above, this yields for the different components
$$
\begin{aligned}
    E^S_x(\mathbf{x}_p) &= 
            \sum_{i,j,k} \arrE^{x}_{\iph,j,k} S^\cell_i(x_p)\,S^\node_j(y_p) \, S^\node_k(z_p) \\
    E^S_y(\mathbf{x}_p) &= 
            \sum_{i,j,k} \arrE^{y}_{i,\jph,k} S^\node_i(x_p)\,S^\cell_j(y_p) \, S^\node_k(z_p) \\
    E^S_z(\mathbf{x}_p) &= 
            \sum_{i,j,k} \arrE^{z}_{i,j,\kph} S^\node_i(x_p)\,S^\node_j(y_p) \, S^\cell_k(z_p)\\
\end{aligned}
$$

In the same way the discrete magnetic field $\bB$ is characterized by its fluxes through the faces of the primal grid, 
defined in Section~\ref{sec:dofs_and_reductions}.

In the variational GEMPIC scheme the particles are coupled with a spline-smoothed magnetic field 
which involves the primal degrees of freedom $\arrB \in \cC_2$
and the reduction $\tcR_1$ of the (vectorized) particle splines, namely:
$$
B^S_d(\mathbf{x}_p)
% \mathbf{e}_{d} \cdot \bB^S(\mathbf{x}_p) 
:=  \arrB \cdot \tcR_1\big(\mathbf{e}_{d} \, S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p)\big)
= \sum_{\texttt{f} \in \Big\{\substack{\text{faces} \\ \text{normal to $\mathbf{e}_{d}$}}\Big\}} \arrB_{\texttt{f}}^d \tcR_1\big(\mathbf{e}_{d} \, S^\part_h(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_p)\big)_{\tilde{\texttt{e}}(\texttt{f})}
$$
again for all dimensions $d \in \{x,y,z\}$, 
and where for each primal face $\texttt{f}$ we have now denoted by $\tilde{\texttt{e}}(\texttt{f})$ its dual edge.

Using the formulas above for the dual dofs of the spline particles, this yields for the different components
$$
\begin{aligned}
    B^S_x(\mathbf{x}_p) &= \sum_{i,j,k} \arrB^{x}_{i,\jph,\kph} \,S^\node_i(x_p)\,S^\cell_j(y_p) \, S^\cell_k(z_p) \\
    B^S_y(\mathbf{x}_p) &= \sum_{i,j,k} \arrB^{y}_{\iph,j,\kph} \,S^\cell_i(x_p)\,S^\node_j(y_p) \, S^\cell_k(z_p) \\
    B^S_z(\mathbf{x}_p) &= \sum_{i,j,k} \arrB^{z}_{\iph,\jph,k} \,S^\cell_i(x_p)\,S^\cell_j(y_p) \, S^\node_k(z_p). \\
\end{aligned}
$$

\section{Exact computation of time integrated $J_x$, $v_{p,x}B_y(x_p)$ and $v_{p,x}B_z(x_p)$ }

One step of the hamiltonian splitting algorithm consists of pushing the particles successively in the three axes and updating positions, velocities and electric field, leaving the magnetic field constant.
Let us describe this algorithm when the particles are pushed in the $x$-direction. The two other directions are handled similarly.  

In this step the following system needs to be solved
$$
\begin{aligned}
    \fract{x_p}{t} &= v_{p,x} \\
    \fract{v_{p, y}}{t} &= - \frac{q_p}{m_p} v_{p,x}(t)B^S_z(x_p(t) ,y_p,z_p) \\
    \fract{v_{p, z}}{t} &= + \frac{q_p}{m_p} v_{p,x}B^S_y(x_p(t),y_p,z_p) \\
    \fract{\tilde{\arrD}^{x}_{\iph,j,k}}{t} &= -\tilde{\arrJ}^{x}_{\iph,j,k}. %\\ 
\end{aligned}
$$
that is,
$$
\begin{aligned}
    \fract{x_p}{t} &= v_{p,x} \\
    \fract{v_{p, y}}{t} &= -\frac{q_p}{m_p} v_{p,x} \sum_{i,j,k} \arrB^{z}_{\iph,\jph,k} \,S^\cell_i(x_p(t))\,S^\cell_j(y_p) \, S^\node_k(z_p)\\
    \fract{v_{p, z}}{t} &= +\frac{q_p}{m_p} v_{p,x} \sum_{i,j,k} \arrB^{y}_{\iph,j,\kph} \,S^\cell_i(x_p(t))\,S^\node_j(y_p) \, S^\cell_k(z_p) \\
    \fract{\tilde{\arrD}^{x}_{\iph,j,k}}{t} &= - \sum_p q_p w_p v_{p,x} S^\cell_i(x_p(t))\, S^\node_j(y_p) \,S^\node_k(z_p)    
\end{aligned}
$$
while the other variables, not appearing on the left-hand side, stay constant.
Using the first equation $v_{p,x}=\fract{x_p}{t}$, we can write the others as exact time derivatives 
by introducing the primitive of the univariate spline $S^{d_x-1}$ on the reference grid,
that we denote by $\check{S}^{d_x-1}$.
The integration constant is irrelevant as only differences of these primitives will be needed.
Writing
$$
S^\prim_i(x) := \check{S}^{d_x-1}\left(\frac{x-x_\iph}{\Delta x}\right)
$$
gives indeed
$$
\fract{}{t} \Big(S^\prim_i(x_p(t))\Big)
= \frac{v_{p,x}}{\Delta x} S^{d_x-1}\left(\frac{x_p(t)-x_\iph}{\Delta x}\right)
= v_{p,x} S^\cell_i(x_p(t))
$$ 
so that the above system becomes
$$
\begin{aligned}
    \fract{x_p}{t} &= v_{p,x} \\
    \fract{v_{p, y}}{t} &= -\fract{}{t} \left(\frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i,j,k} \arrB^{z}_{\iph,\jph,k} \,S^\prim_i(x_p(t))\,S^\cell_j(y_p) \, S^\node_k(z_p)\right)\\
    \fract{v_{p, z}}{t} &= +\fract{}{t} \left(\frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i,j,k} \arrB^{y}_{\iph,j,\kph} \,S^\prim_i(x_p(t))\,S^\node_j(y_p) \, S^\cell_k(z_p) \right)\\
    \fract{\tilde{\arrD}^{x}_{\iph,j,k}}{t} &= - \fract{}{t}\left(\sum_p q_p w_p S^\prim_i(x_p(t))\, S^\node_j(y_p) \,S^\node_k(z_p) \right)   
\end{aligned}
$$
Then, integrating the original system in time between $t_0$ and $t_1$ yields without further approximation

$$
\begin{aligned}
    x_p&(t_1) = x_p(t_0) + (t_1-t_0) v_{p,x} \\
    v_{p, y}&(t_1) = v_{y,p}(t_0) -  
    \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i,j,k} \arrB^{z}_{\iph,\jph,k} \big[S^\prim_i(x_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1} S^\cell_j(y_p) \, S^\node_k(z_p)    
    \\
    v_{p, z}&(t_1) =  v_{z,p}(t_0) + 
    \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i,j,k} \arrB^{y}_{\iph,j,\kph} \big[S^\prim_i(x_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1}  S^\node_j(y_p) \, S^\cell_k(z_p)
    \\
    \tilde{\arrD}^{x}_{\iph,j,k} & (t_1) =  \tilde{\arrD}^{x}_{\iph,j,k}(t_0) - 
    \sum_p q_p w_p \big[S^\prim_i(x_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1}  S^\node_j(y_p) \,S^\node_k(z_p).
\end{aligned}
$$
where $\big[S^\prim_i(x_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1} = S^\prim_i(x_p(t_1))-S^\prim_i(x_p(t_0))$ is the primitive difference.

\subsection{2D version}

Using the same strategy as for the 3D version, the system becomes

$$
\begin{aligned}

    \fract{v_{p, z}}{t} &= +\fract{}{t} \left(\frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i,j} \arrB^{y}_{\iph,j} \,S^\prim_i(x_p(t))\,S^\node_j(y_p) \right)\\
    \fract{\tilde{\arrD}^{x}_{\iph,j}}{t} &= - \fract{}{t}\left(\sum_p q_p w_p S^\prim_i(x_p(t))\, S^\node_j(y_p) \right)

$$
Then, integrating the original system in time between $t_0$ and $t_1$ yields without further approximation

$$
\begin{aligned}
    x_p&(t_1) = x_p(t_0) + (t_1-t_0) v_{p,x} \\
    v_{p, y}&(t_1) = v_{y,p}(t_0) -  
    \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i,j} \arrB^{z}_{\iph,\jph} \big[S^\prim_i(x_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1} S^\cell_j(y_p)    
    \\
    v_{p, z}&(t_1) =  v_{z,p}(t_0) + 
    \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i,j} \arrB^{y}_{\iph,j} \big[S^\prim_i(x_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1}  S^\node_j(y_p) 
    \\
    \tilde{\arrD}^{x}_{\iph,j} & (t_1) =  \tilde{\arrD}^{x}_{\iph,j}(t_0) - 
    \sum_p q_p w_p \big[S^\prim_i(x_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1}  S^\node_j(y_p).
\end{aligned}
$$

Keeping only $\mathcal{H}_{p,y}$, the above steps lead to

$$
\begin{aligned}
    y_p&(t_1) = y_p(t_0) + (t_1-t_0) v_{p,y} \\
    v_{p,z}&(t_1) = v_{z,p}(t_0) -  
    \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i,j} \arrB^{x}_{i,\jph} S^\node_i(x_p) \big[S^\prim_j(y_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1}     
    \\
    v_{p,x}&(t_1) =  v_{x,p}(t_0) + 
    \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i,j} \arrB^{z}_{\iph,\jph} S^\cell_i(x_p) \big[S^\prim_j(y_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1}   
    \\
    \tilde{\arrD}^{y}_{i,\jph} & (t_1) =  \tilde{\arrD}^{y}_{i,\jph}(t_0) - 
    \sum_p q_p w_p S^\node_i(x_p) \big[S^\prim_j(y_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Since particles have no z=$z$ coordinates, the system for keeping only $\mathcal{H}_{p,z}$, the system to solve becomes

$$
\begin{aligned}
    \fract{v_{p,x}}{t} &= -\frac{q_p}{m_p} v_{p,z} \sum_{i,j} \arrB^{y}_{\iph,j} \,S^\cell_i(x_p)\,S^\node_j(y_p)\\
    \fract{v_{p,y}}{t} &= +\frac{q_p}{m_p} v_{p,z} \sum_{i,j} \arrB^{x}_{i,\jph} \,S^\node_i(x_p)\,S^\cell_j(y_p)\\
    \fract{\tilde{\arrD}^{z}_{i,j}}{t} &= - \sum_p q_p w_p v_{p,z} S^\node_i(x_p)\, S^\node_j(y_p)  
\end{aligned}
$$

Since the right hand side of the system is constant in this step, the numerical scheme results to 

$$
\begin{aligned}
    v_{p,x}&(t_1) = v_{x,p}(t_0) -  
    (t_1 - t_0) v_{p,z} \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i,j} \arrB^{y}_{\iph,j} S^\cell_i(x_p) S^\node_j(y_p)     
    \\
    v_{p,y}&(t_1) =  v_{y,p}(t_0) + 
    (t_1 - t_0) v_{p,z} \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i,j} \arrB^{x}_{i,\jph} S^\node_i(x_p) S^\cell_j(y_p)   
    \\
    \tilde{\arrD}^{z}_{i,j} & (t_1) =  \tilde{\arrD}^{z}_{i,j}(t_0) - 
    \sum_p (t_1 - t_0) \, v_{p,z} \,q_p w_p \,S^\node_i(x_p) \, S^\node_j(y_p).
\end{aligned}
$$

\subsection{1D version}
For keeping only $\mathcal{H}_{p,x}$, the following system needs to be solved

$$
\begin{aligned}
    \fract{x_p}{t} &= v_{p,x} \\
    \fract{v_{p,y}}{t} &= -\frac{q_p}{m_p} v_{p,x} \sum_{i} \arrB^{z}_{\iph} \,S^\cell_i(x_p(t))\\
    \fract{v_{p,z}}{t} &= +\frac{q_p}{m_p} v_{p,x} \sum_{i} \arrB^{y}_{\iph} \,S^\cell_i(x_p(t))\\
    \fract{\tilde{\arrD}^{x}_{\iph}}{t} &= - \sum_p q_p w_p v_{p,x} S^\cell_i(x_p(t)) 
\end{aligned}
$$

Similar to the 3- and 2-D version, this yields the numerical scheme

$$
\begin{aligned}
    x_p&(t_1) = x_p(t_0) + (t_1-t_0) v_{p,x} \\
    v_{p, y}&(t_1) = v_{y,p}(t_0) -  
    \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i} \arrB^{z}_{\iph} \big[S^\prim_i(x_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1}    
    \\
    v_{p, z}&(t_1) =  v_{z,p}(t_0) + 
    \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i} \arrB^{y}_{\iph} \big[S^\prim_i(x_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1}  
    \\
    \tilde{\arrD}^{x}_{\iph} & (t_1) =  \tilde{\arrD}^{x}_{\iph}(t_0) - 
    \sum_p q_p w_p \big[S^\prim_i(x_p)\big]_{t_0}^{t_1} .
\end{aligned}
$$

Since particles have no $y$ coordinates, the system for keeping only $\mathcal{H}_{p,y}$ becomes

$$
\begin{aligned}
    \fract{v_{p,z}}{t} &= -\frac{q_p}{m_p} v_{p,y} \sum_{i} \arrB^{x}_{i} \,S^\node_i(x_p)\\
    \fract{v_{p,x}}{t} &= +\frac{q_p}{m_p} v_{p,y} \sum_{i} \arrB^{x}_{\iph} S^\cell_i(x_p)\\
    \fract{\tilde{\arrD}^{y}_{i}}{t} &= - \sum_p q_p w_p v_{p,y} S^\node_i(x_p)  
\end{aligned}
$$

With the same argument as for the z-Direction in 2D, this yields

$$
\begin{aligned}
    v_{p,z}&(t_1) = v_{p,z}(t_0) -  
    (t_1 - t_0) v_{p,y} \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i} \arrB^{x}_{i} S^\node_i(x_p)     
    \\
    v_{p,x}&(t_1) =  v_{p,x}(t_0) + 
    (t_1 - t_0) v_{p,z} \frac{q_p}{m_p} \sum_{i} \arrB^{z}_{\iph} S^\cell_i(x_p)   
    \\
    \tilde{\arrD}^{y}_{i} & (t_1) =  \tilde{\arrD}^{y}_{i}(t_0) - 
    \sum_p (t_1 - t_0) \, v_{p,y} \,q_p w_p \,S^\node_i(x_p).
\end{aligned}
$$

For keeping only $\mathcal{H}_{p,z}$, the system works the same as in the y-direction.
